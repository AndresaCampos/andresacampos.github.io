automate and scale CRISP's customer onboarding process. CRISP model iteration requires labeled contracts—contracts where each term has a ground-truth classification (Standard, Non-Standard, or Missing) assigned by the customer. In practice, customers often cannot provide labeled contracts, and instead review CRISP's predictions directly, correcting where they disagree. A single batch generates hundreds of classification results, and the customer cannot review all of them—yet every distinct classification pattern needs coverage, because a pattern the customer never sees is a pattern we never get feedback on. I owned: scientific problem formulation, algorithm design, high-level system architecture, evaluation strategy design, cross-team requirements clarification (authored the CRISP Terms Requirements Guidelines to resolve engineering team confusion about onboarding inputs), and implementation (in progress). Technical Approach Formulated the problem as pool-based active learning: predictions are the unlabeled pool, the customer is the oracle, and each review item is a query. Designed a two-phase clustering algorithm: cosine similarity as a cheap first pass to form candidate clusters, followed by LLM validation to confirm semantic equivalence. This architecture addresses a fundamental gap—embedding-based metrics cannot reliably distinguish critical numeric differences in contract language (e.g., "90 days" vs. "120 days" have cosine similarity ~0.96 but are materially different obligations). Designed classification-aware handling: Standard terms cluster into 1–2 large groups (high aggregation), Non-Standard terms form many small clusters and singletons (low aggregation), and Missing terms use template-based grouping or batch confirmation. Ranking applies active learning theory: Standard clusters first by size (information density), Non-Standard clusters by similarity to Standard (uncertainty sampling—borderline cases are most informative), Missing items last (least signal for iteration). Designed two competing LLM validation approaches (pairwise vs. whole-cluster) to be evaluated empirically. Additionally authored the CRISP Terms Requirements Guidelines to resolve cross-team confusion—clarifying that only standard language is required per term, establishing the uniqueness rule (one entry per term per contract category), and defining the contract category structure. Functional Impact Expected Operational Impact: * 85%+ reduction in customer review burden (500 → ~73 review items in representative example) * Label propagation: each customer verdict propagates to all contracts in a cluster, multiplying labeled data per review * Onboarding acceleration: enables feedback collection when customers cannot provide labeled contracts upfront * Adaptive: when language is homogeneous, clustering compresses the queue; when diverse, ranking surfaces the most informative items first Evaluation Strategy (designed, experiments pending): * Experiment 1: Cosine similarity threshold sweep (0.6–0.9) to optimize precision/recall tradeoff * Experiment 2: Pairwise vs. Whole-Cluster LLM validation comparison, stratified by classification type and cluster size * Primary metric: Co-clustering precision (target 100%)—false merges silently corrupt labels, while false splits only cost extra review effort Scientific Innovation Designed a novel two-phase clustering architecture addressing a fundamental gap: embedding-based metrics are dominated by surrounding boilerplate and miss critical numeric variations, while LLM calls are too expensive to use exhaustively. The cosine-then-LLM approach uses permissive thresholds to avoid missing equivalent pairs, then relies on LLM understanding to catch false merges no distance metric can detect. The ranking strategy connects three active learning principles—information density weighting, uncertainty sampling, and batch-mode diversity—to ensure the customer's limited effort always lands where it is most productive for model iteration. In this paper we propose an empirical approach to model selection that explicitly balances parameter bias against model complexity. Our method uses synthetic data to calibrate the relation between bias and the χ2 difference between models. This allows us to interpret χ2 values obtained from real data (even if catalogues are blinded) and choose a model accordingly. We apply our method to the problem of intrinsic alignments -- one of the most significant weak lensing systematics, and a major contributor to the error budget in modern lensing surveys. Specifically, we consider the example of the Dark Energy Survey Year 3 (DES Y3), and compare the commonly used nonlinear alignment (NLA) and tidal alignment & tidal torque (TATT) models. Once noise is accounted for, we find that it is possible to set a threshold Δχ2 that guarantees an analysis using NLA is unbiased at some specified level Nσ and confidence level.
